Wohnscheune

Location: Saargau, Germany
Year: 2021 on going
Project objective: Architectural Draft

The identity of a village is strongly influenced by its environment and history, which in turn shape the village's structure and architectural style. The architecture of the houses in the village is influenced by factors such as climate, locally available building materials, cultural and historical heritage, and economic influences. The village has an identity that connects it with the surrounding landscape, and this connection gives rise to the overall appearance and aesthetic quality of the village.
Only in recent decades have humans had the ability to overcome the constraints of locality. Knowledge and materials are now globally accessible, and traditional construction methods can be replaced by industrial production. There is no longer a necessity to design the village structure based on climate and local needs. However, this progress has resulted in a loss of identity for traditional settlements, and the aesthetic quality and livability of public spaces have been compromised.
This negative trend can only be halted by embracing the principles of vernacular architecture. The first step is to develop a sense of the place, its history, and its identity, in order to analyze what defines the village and its structure. Directing attention to the landscape and the village is essential before focusing on individual houses as projects. Only by doing so can a house be created that harmoniously fits within the village, as each house plays a crucial role in shaping the overall character of the village.
In the Saargau region, where our house is located, the "Trierer Einhaus" is a typical traditional farmhouse and serves as our inspiration for this project. It features an elongated form, with the living house, stable, and barn are aligned. The region was influenced by the Romans, and as a result, the Trierer Einhaus exhibits a clear geometric order in its floor plan and facade.
Our aim for this project is to create a modern interpretation of the Trierer Einhaus that blends seamlessly into the village and landscape. Vernacular architecture does not mean sticking to traditions. Instead, it involves finding the essence of the place while make use of the possibilities offered by the technology of our time.
The site for our project is situated between two big farm houses, built in the 19th-century. Our house is intended to complement this ensemble and resemble a barn. The living barn is designed as a double house, with a corrugated metal facade.
